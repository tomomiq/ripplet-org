title: Avoiding project overload
date: 2022-02-15
description: A story about saying no without making excuses, for people with project overload

---

**Last September, I raised my hand to join a new project and retracted it, five minutes later.** We were six program coordinators on a bi-weekly Zoom call, setting goals and scoping this project. The person who'd proposed it was psyched, pitching ideas and rallying for resources. My experience and skillset was a solid match for the work ahead. It made sense, to me and to everyone, that I would take part.

**Then, I realized I wasn't energized about the project.**

The work was too familiar to me. It wasn't the experience that I was seeking with this particular group. I knew I'd frustrate myself with my half-assed-ness and feel like I let everyone down.

**I declared that I didn't want to work on it.**

Working through my unease, I was able to to find the words to explain that **this wasn't why I was here**, that what I wanted to do was X instead. And I apologized and offered to be in a support role, which was immediately and heartily welcomed. The discussion moved onto next steps and ended on a high note.

I've been fulfilling this support role since, motivated to contribute in discrete ways that the project lead can run with. I feel at a greater ease in general, knowing that

* My expertise can still effectively benefit the group
* I will have capacity for X when the time comes
* We as a group can tend to our personal intentions and the program will be better for it

**What would happen if we deliberately stepped aside more, instead of going with the flow?**

It's not often an obvious choice, especially in the moment. A *I'll see what happens* can lead to interesting developments. But sometimes, stepping aside is what will protect the space for what we know matters to us.
