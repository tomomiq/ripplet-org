I understand this structure to be a workshop-friendly take on [Appreciative Inquiry](<https://appreciativeinquiry.champlain.edu/learn/appreciative-inquiry-introduction/>), and often use it as a collective sense-making step early in the process of identifying the change we want to bring forth.

Sidebar: The first time I did something similar was the Stanford Design School’s [crash course](<https://dschool.stanford.edu/resources/gear-up-how-to-kick-off-a-crash-course>) on Design Thinking about re-designing the gift giving experience. I remember being amazed that one seemingly banal story (about the last time you selected a gift for someone) could open the door to so many ideas, once I started listening with a different kind of ear. 

## Facilitation notes

Inquiring into first-person accounts of what’s already working quickly highlights weak signals that can be extrapolated into a bigger story. The cross-storytelling generates warm energy in the group dynamics too, as everyone’s contribution is weaved into the conversation and the group recognizes, _hey, we’ve got this_. 

I like to explicitly prompt the interviewer to open the exchange by asking their partner, "Tell me a story about..." The desired effect is to re-inforce a) that it's time to share a story, not ideas or opinions b) the question-and-answer dynamic. 

## Workshop design notes

Sometimes I’ll ask that the outputs of the interview be expressed as storyboards, so that we’re breaking down the behaviors that make up conditions of success.
