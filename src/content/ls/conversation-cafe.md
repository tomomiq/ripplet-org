Conversation cafe is quite general and can be applied to most topics that are meant to be worked on in conversation-based breakouts groups. 

## In practice

I've had the most success with prompts that elicit personal stories, where an intimate exchange within a small group is a self-contained activity. People are happy to have a meaningful connection, and don't feel like they've missed out on what other groups discussed. If they’re curious, reading the per-group slide or flipchart of discussion points is enough.

## Facilitator notes

This structure is a great one for quickly deepening conversations. When run online, it's usually done in breakout rooms and requires someone in each group to be a strict timekeeper. In addition, even at one minute each, multiple rounds around the table takes longer than a freefall discussion. It becomes tempting for both the facilitator who is managing a tight agenda and for impatient participants in a breakout room, to throw the constraints out the window. Stay strong. 

## Workshop design notes

If it's a prompt where participants do benefit from being exposed to what other groups worked on, the structure should be followed by a harvesting or cross-pollination activity. A common workshop design flaw is to skimp on time to download breakout outputs and discuss them at the room level. 

This is a minor point but I usually re-name this activity to something a little less casual-sounding (ex. Roundtable discussions) or incorporate the content (ex. Your experience with xxx) to better suit the aesthetics of my workshops.
