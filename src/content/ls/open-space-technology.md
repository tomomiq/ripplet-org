My first experience at an unconference must have been about ten years ago. The organizers enthusiastically invited first-time attendees to organize a session - that it was a tradition. While the idea of standing in front of a roomful of strangers made my stomach turn, I thought I should - that I had to. It was a tradition! 

I was so pumped that people came to our session, that I had things to say that people wanted to listen to and that we had a lively exchange. I treasure this memory because it forever changed my perspective of what an event could _feel_ like. And that a group could self-facilitate a learning experience, solely based on the knowledge, experience and attention of who was in that room, was priceless. I went home and promptly organized an event at work to learn how to host the whole shindig myself. 

At that time of my life, I had dove headfirst in the world of Creative Commons, open-source software and citizen journalism. The unconference format seemed perfectly at home in these movements. Years later, I was surprised to encounter OST in the fields of agile development and organizational culture change. And a few years after that, OST popped up in the list of Liberating Structures that I was thumbing through, and I thought - of course. This is a Structure that Liberates.
